Technology For Humankind

UXD Experience Studio | Spring 2021


Project Space

Technology for Humankind tasked us with simplifying their setup process for their Friendship Lamps. These lamps connect to Wi-Fi and allow people to communicate to loved ones by simply tapping their lamp. When tapped, the paired lamp(s) light up with an assigned color, showing who is sent the gesture. The setup process can be frustrating for people with low technical literacy if network issues pop up, or if they arrive at a point in the setup process that is beyond their understanding or experience level. This frustration hinders Humankind’s goal of creating positive interactions between people if the experience of using the lamps begins with issues getting started.

User Group

The user group that we focused on was customers with low technical literacy, specifically, people who have limited experience setting up Wi-Fi Internet of Things devices. Humankind has found that this group often needs assistance setting up their devices, so they wanted us to have the setup process take their needs into account.

Deliverables

  • Research, including a competitive analysis, secondary research, and user testing with the lamps.
  • Low-fidelity prototypes for a mobile application setup process. These mobile app prototypes will be low-fidelity mockups of an application, meaning that they will show the content and look of the application but no interactivity.

Current Set-up Process

There are currently three setup options for the Friendship Lamp: Bridge Setup, Automatic Setup (Windows 10 only), and Direct to Wifi Setup. Our team was tasked with improving the direct to wifi setup because it causes the most issues for customers according to our sponsor.

Understanding our User Group

A literary analysis was conducted to learn more about different setup processes and identify issues with the current setup process for the Filimin Friendship Lamps. Two main topics researched were: other setup guides that are wifi-related to analyze their setup processes and ways to help people who have low technical literacy understand things that require technical literacy.

Based on the findings, an affinity diagram – which is a way to organize research findings

into categories – was created in order to organize different ways to help people with low technical literacy. The guiding questions for our secondary research were as follows:

  • How to accommodate people with low technical literacy?
  • How people can gain technical literacy, how to quickly inform people without overwhelming them?
  • How to simplify the understanding of technical processes?

Takeaways

Insights from literary analysis that help people who have low technical literacy:

  • Using visuals and having clear and concise instructions are critical for people with low technical literacy
  • Instructional videos that work through the process with the user
  • Simplifying any jargon language helps accommodate people with low technical literacy.

The team assessed the current Filimin Friendship Lamp setup process and identified areas where the setup process could help people with low technical literacy. Two specific areas that could be improved are the unpackaging of the box to emphasize there are two ways to set up the lamp and clearer instruction when connecting to the lamp wifi and then reconnecting back to regular wifi.

Exploring Set-up Processes

A competitive analysis was conducted to see different setup processes Filimin’s competitors and other companies use to understand what aspects could help or hinder users with low technical literacy. The team researched the current Filimin set-up, two direct competitors, and then six additional set-up processes for different products. The additional device set-up processes were included to show how other devices lay out their set-up processes. The research was then synthesised to focus on the main features that create a more comprehensible set-up process helping to support future ideation.

Link to competitive analysis image

Takeaways

The most beneficial features that aided users were those that included visuals (i.e. videos, pictures, icons) along with having detailed step-by-step instructions. The competitive analysis helped in brainstorming alterations to the current set-up process layout that would be the most beneficial for those with low technical literacy. These main takeaways influenced the initial sketches and will continue to be reflected on with further ideation.

Gaining Knowledge of First Hand Issues

Goals

For our first round of primary research for this project, interviews were conducted with Customer Support Representatives from Technology for Humankind and customers of the Filimin Friendship Lamps to gain a better understanding of recurring issues with the setup process of the Friendship Lamps.

Customer Support Representatives – interview goals:

  • Gain an understanding of what issues the Customer Service Reps have to deal with
  • Gain any information about their experiences while helping customers.

Filmin customers – interview goals:

  • Find out where they struggled with the Friendship Lamp setup process
  • Better understand their level of technical literacy.

Interviews

The interviews were used to give direction to what parts of the set-up process needed to be altered to better accommodate users who have limited experience with connecting Internet of Things devices.

The questions for the customer service representatives focused on their experiences with helping customers over the phone and the common issues that were brought up while talking to these customers. The questions for the Filmin customers focused on their experience setting up the Filimin Lamps and also focused on revealing points of confusion they experienced.

Link to Interview affinity diagram image

Takeaways

Common issues customers experience:

  • Connecting the lamps to wifi
  • Issues with the bridge (an alternate setup that plugs directly into the wifi router):
    • Malfunctioning
    • Not understanding the purpose of it
    • Thought it should be optional because they preferred the wifi-setup process
  • Website confusion:
    • Lack of explanation of what certain terms mean
    • Confusion about the color customization

Insights from Customer Service Representatives:

  • Intentionally use visuals
  • Be personable and patient with customers
  • Customers shared the wifi-setup was very easy when a customer service rep walked them through it

Making the options for the set up clear, simplifying the terms and words on the website, and changing the layout of the color customizations are all ideas to take away from the interviews. These insights were taken into sketching and prototyping, and the focus was on addressing some of these pain points.

Exploring Concepts & Ideas

Goals

In order to explore ideas based on findings from our research, interviews, and competitive analysis, we did some sketching with the specific aim of making the setup process more accessible and easy to use for users with low technical literacy. Team members created individual sketches using takeaways from our interviews and preliminary secondary research, focusing on accommodating for low technical literacy.

The main takeaways that we used as inspiration for our sketching were:

  • Issues with connecting lamp to wifi
  • Unclear instructions (especially relating to wifi connection steps)
  • Confusion about color meanings & color selection
  • High quality visuals and walkthrough videos are very helpful
  • Simple language and clear concise steps are key

The idea 2 sketch represents a new way to format paper instructions to show the two separate methods and prioritizes the direct-to-wifi method which interviewees suggested was the easier method.

It also includes images of what parts of the product are necessary for each method.

Idea 3 represents a more detailed and simply worded explanation of the wifi-connection steps.

Takeaways

  • Linked resources are a good way to accommodate for low technical literacy without cluttering the written steps or bothering people who do not need the extra resources
    • They strike a good balance between not enough information and too much information
  • There needs to be a clear differentiation between the direct to wifi and bridge setup instructions
  • The colors of the lamp and how to set them up should be clarified and simplified
  • More visual aids, images, and videos can help more than written instruction
  • Limit words – only include what is necessary

Refining Our Ideas

Our goal for this round of sketching was to narrow down and refine some of the key ideas we came up with from our initial sketches.

Takeaways

  • Consider having more “need help” links tailored to each step – this makes the information accessible without cluttering it for more experienced users
  • Breaking the steps down page by page makes them more clear and understandable
  • Need to simplify and explain the color selection
  • Including more visuals and less words is key for increasing understanding of each step and how to complete it

Based on these takeaways as well as the takeaways from our first set of sketches, we began creating clearer and more defined sketches and paper prototypes that utilize these ideas. Our goal was to hone in on and visualize key features and changes that would assist low technical literacy users without getting in the way of users who have a better understanding of the technology.

Adding Color Selection and Dashboard

After creating wireframes based on our paper prototype, we decided to create a color selection screen that’s included in the setup process as well as a dashboard that users are sent to after the setup process is finished. A color selector was created because users did not understand the purpose of selecting colors. A dashboard was created to help guide users on what features the dashboard has. Previously, the dashboard had no guidance and users did not utilize features like changing the lamp color or the amount of time their lamp can stay on because they did not know these features existed. Evaluating the User’s Understanding

Teach Back Testing

We conducted teach back testing on three individuals to see if our version of the setup process is beneficial for our users, and if the images included in the wireframes provided helpful guidance. The overall process of setting up Friendship Lamps was explained to the users. The users were instructed to go page by page to engage with the wireframes in order to complete tasks. We learned from the user what they expected from each page and if they understood the features of it. This will help us identify if the changes that we made to the setup process were useful, as well as guide any changes we make going forward.

Takeaways

Some of the main takeaways we received from testing were as follows. These helped inform the iterations you will see in the next section.

  • Groups
    • Misunderstood what groups are
    • Didn’t understand that the buttons were supposed to toggle
    • Thought groups were to find new people interested in the same thing
    • Knew that groups connected lamps, but not that it was to connect w family
  • Could add key phrases/ takeaways
  • Couldn’t easily think of a lamp name
  • Suggested to explain what everything was before the setup process
  • Device ID was unclear (need a better picture)
  • Wording of disconnecting from Filimin wifi was confusing, didn’t understand the purpose of connecting and disconnecting from wifi
  • Did not understand that the white circle can be moved for the color customizer
  • Going forward iterations need to take in account users who struggled while navigating the setup process

Exploring Potentials Issues in the Set-up Process

Goal

After a feedback session with our professors, we decided to look into the possible edge cases that our users might encounter when using our designed app. We define edge cases as issues that occur infrequently or to a small number of users, but still need to be accounted for in our designs. The purpose of designing for these edge cases is to help users with low technical literacy recover from issues on their own, without needing to contact customer support.

Method

In order to go more in depth on each section of the app, we split up into teams focusing on each step of the setup process. We went through each of our assigned pages and identified what could go wrong for the user at each moment in the app. We then addressed these issues by iterating on the current mockup.

Evaluating our Edge Cases

Scenario Testing

Scenario testing involves creating hypothetical situations for the user to engage with our prototypes. We used our edge cases to create the scenarios to determine whether or not our iterations helped with those troubleshooting errors. We tested three people in our user group by showing them the screens, reading the scenario to them, and letting them determine what steps they would take to complete the task. We followed each completed task up with questions regarding their thought process and intentions. We also presented this testing to a customer support rep at Filimin to evaluate whether our iterations were helpful regarding the problems customers typically face.

Takeaways

These are the takeaways from the customer support rep at Filimin:

  • We also have options for support contact via email and chat
  • Once the user inputs their wifi information, the user’s phone disconnects from the lamp, and the setup process has to restart if the info is wrong
  • It is rare for people to not have their network show up from the dropdown
  • Having a step to check if the lamp is online is important

These are the takeaways from the users we tested with:

  • Users felt confused about what the wifi was and why it should have connected automatically.
  • Users were initially confused about the amount of small text on the groups page.
  • Users were confused by the options on the color selection page thinking that range of colors referred to both of the color sliders rather than just the circular range of color sliders.
  • Users initially had a hard time seeing certain buttons and help links.

Analyzing our Design Through Heuristics

Heuristic Analysis

Our overall goals for this method were to ensure that our design met usability and accessibility standards and would be optimized for use by our user group, who come from older demographics and have unique needs that need to be considered especially with readability of text and manual dexterity. We used a heuristic analysis, also referred to as a heuristic evaluation, in order to evaluate our design and ensure that it meets usability standards and the heuristics for older individuals.

“Heuristic evaluation involves having a small set of evaluators examine the interface and judge its compliance with recognized usability principles (the “heuristics”)” (Chisnell et al., 2006). The main goal of a heuristic analysis is to discover usability problems in order to address them and make the interface easier for users to interact with. For the heuristic analysis we used a selection of heuristics developed for older adults, which addressed the needs of our user group who are mostly from an older demographic. These heuristics include:

  • Readability (text size, placement)
  • Interactive elements (buttons, dropdowns, menus)
  • Interface text (text color, size, meeting AA / AAA standards)
  • Error messages (placement, clarity, colors, wording)

Takeaways

  • The overall readability was poor because our text sizes were too small for older generations.
  • Interactive elements: buttons, menus, and button cushion were lacking. We fixed this by including more button cushion and making buttons larger an easier to click
  • Interface text was hard to read due to the size and color. We fixed this by bolding certain words to make the readability easier.
  • Error Messages were placed in purposeful locations and had simple explanatory messages to help guide people on what to do if something was not working properly.
